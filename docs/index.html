Understanding Student Mental Health

Across India, many university students balance their studies with family expectations and personal goals. While this dedication often leads to academic success, our research shows it may also impact mental wellbeing.

In India's educational environment, students work hard to achieve good grades. However, their mental health needs often receive less attention. Our project examines data from nearly 28,000 university students across India to better understand this issue and identify what factors might contribute to student depression.

"Academic success is important, but student wellbeing matters too."

We chose this topic because student mental health is an important issue that needs more attention. Our data shows that more than 60% of students reported symptoms of depression. As students ourselves, we understand these challenges and wanted to create visualizations that help explain the different factors affecting mental health in educational settings.

About This Project

Our analysis uses the Student Depression Dataset from Kaggle. This dataset includes information about students' mental health, academic performance (CGPA), lifestyle habits (sleep patterns, social media usage), demographics (age, gender), and other mental health indicators (suicidal thoughts, family history of mental illness). Through our visualizations, we aim to show the human story behind the statistics and help identify warning signs of depression among students.

Understanding Our Data

Before creating visualizations, we wanted to understand what our dataset really represents. Each row in our data shows one student's life - their sleep habits, stress levels, and mental health. Each number tells part of a story about nearly 28,000 university students across India. Like most real-world data, these needed to be prepared carefully.

The dataset had no missing values, which is unusual and helpful. However, many columns had long labels and text responses instead of numbers. To make the data easier to analyze while keeping its meaning intact, we made several improvements.

How We Prepared the Data

  • Making column names simpler: We changed long names like "Have you ever had suicidal thoughts?" to shorter ones like Suicidal_Thoughts.
  • Converting text to numbers: Yes/No answers became 1 for "Yes" and 0 for "No." We did this for columns like Suicidal_Thoughts and Family_History.
  • Creating numerical scales: For categories like Financial_Stress, we used numbers (Low = 1, Medium = 2, High = 3) to make comparisons easier.
  • Standardizing time measurements: Sleep descriptions like "5–6 hours" became numeric values (5 hours) for consistency.
  • Creating separate columns for categories: Variables like Gender and Profession were split into multiple yes/no columns for better analysis.

These changes made the dataset much easier to work with, allowing us to better explore how different aspects of student life relate to mental health.

Depression Among Students: The Numbers

When we analyzed our data, one finding stood out: a significant number of students report experiencing depression. The statistics show this is an important issue that needs attention.

Mental Health Concerns

More than 60% of students in our survey reported depression symptoms. This percentage is higher than what's typically found in the general population. This suggests that the university environment may create specific challenges for mental health.

50% of students show signs of both depression and suicidal thoughts, placing them in a high-risk category. Another 8.5% experience depression without suicidal thoughts, putting them in a moderate risk group. Only 41.5% of students reported no depression symptoms.

"Our educational systems need to balance academic excellence with student wellbeing."

Education Level Analysis

Depression rates vary across different education levels. Undergraduate students show higher rates of depression, possibly due to the challenges of adjusting to university life and managing new academic expectations. This visualization helps identify when students might need the most support.

What Influences Student Mental Health?

Several factors affect student mental health, from daily habits to academic pressures. In this section, we explore how different aspects of student life relate to depression, revealing potential risk factors and protective measures.

We invite you to explore the interactive visualization above. It shows how different factors in a student's life connect to their mental health.

Looking at the data this way reveals clear patterns. For example, we found that longer work hours link to higher depression rates. For each additional hour of work, depression likelihood increases by about 5% until reaching 9 hours, where it remains consistently high.

Interestingly, we also found that students who report no academic pressure actually have a higher chance of depression than those with low academic pressure. This suggests that some level of academic engagement may be beneficial for mental health.

The Strongest Connections

While our interactive visualization shows trends for each factor, we wanted to know which factors have the strongest link to depression. We used statistical measures including Cramer's V and point-biserial correlation to find out. The results are shown below.

The chart shows that academic pressure has the strongest connection to depression, followed by financial stress.

Other Important Factors

While academic and financial stress show the strongest links to depression, we also found that sleep patterns and dietary habits play important roles. These are areas where students might have more control, offering potential ways to improve mental wellbeing even in demanding academic environments.

Diet and Family History

Our analysis revealed an interesting relationship between diet, family history of mental illness, and depression. The chart below shows this connection:

The data shows that students with both unhealthy diets and a family history of mental illness have the highest depression risk scores (0.73). Even students with no family history but unhealthy diets show elevated depression risk (0.68). This suggests that while genetics play a role, lifestyle choices are also important.

Encouragingly, healthy dietary habits appear to provide some protection even for students with a family history of mental illness, with risk scores much lower (0.48) than those with poor diets. This finding suggests a practical way for students to potentially improve their mental health.

Regional Patterns: Depression Across India

Where students study may affect how they feel. We mapped depression rates across Indian cities using our survey data. Each dot on the map represents a city, colored from green to red based on how many students reported depression symptoms. Darker red means more students in that location reported suicidal thoughts.

Interactive map: Click on a city to see depression rates and student counts.

The map shows clear patterns of mental health across India. Many large cities show higher rates of student depression. In some urban areas, more than half of the surveyed students reported depression symptoms.

While this map doesn't explain why students in certain areas struggle more, it provides valuable geographic insights. Cities with more stressful environments—whether from higher living costs, academic competition, or fewer support systems—often show more depression cases on our map.

As you explore the map, you can see these patterns emerge. Each dot represents real students who face both academic demands and mental health challenges.

Suggestions for Improvement

Based on our data analysis, we suggest several approaches that could help address mental health issues among Indian students. These recommendations are for different groups in the education system.

For Educational Institutions

Universities and colleges should make mental health a priority by creating accessible support services and awareness programs. Since poor sleep links strongly to depression, institutions could review assignment schedules and exam timings to allow for healthier sleep patterns. Adding stress management and emotional resilience training to the curriculum could provide students with practical coping skills.

For Students

Getting enough sleep appears especially important for mental health, with our data showing much lower depression rates among students who sleep 7-8 hours regularly. Limiting social media use and building supportive friendships also links to better mental health. Students should learn to recognize early signs of depression and seek help early, as this can prevent more serious problems.

For Parents and Families

Families play an important role in supporting student mental health. Our data suggests that high parental expectations about grades may contribute to depression in some cases. Parents should keep communication open about mental health, learn to recognize warning signs, and balance academic goals with overall wellbeing. Creating a home environment where students feel comfortable discussing mental health challenges helps ensure they get support when needed.

"Success in education should include both academic achievement and good mental health."
